How discount caps affect the ranking

A discount cap is a maximum PKR saving per transaction, and it's the main reason a lower headline percentage can still save you more money than a higher one. Understanding how caps work makes the bill-size slider in the tool a lot more meaningful.

What a cap actually means in practice

When a bank says "20% off, up to Rs 500", the cap is Rs 500. That means on a Rs 2,500 bill, you save exactly Rs 500, the full 20%. On a Rs 4,000 bill, you still save Rs 500, now effectively 12.5%. On a Rs 8,000 bill, the saving is still Rs 500, now just 6.25%. The cap has completely flattened the discount.

Compare that to a card offering "15% off, up to Rs 1,500". On a Rs 2,500 bill you save Rs 375, less than the capped card. But on a Rs 4,000 bill you save Rs 600. On a Rs 8,000 bill you save Rs 1,200. The lower-percentage card has quietly become much more valuable for anyone dining at higher bill sizes.

This is why the ranking changes when you move the bill slider. It is not a bug. It is the most honest thing the tool does.

Where caps typically kick in

Published caps often start in the low hundreds of rupees and move into four digits on stronger premium offers. At the lower end, a Rs 300 cap plateaus almost immediately. A 25% discount capped at Rs 300 maxes out at a bill of only Rs 1,200. Above that, you are getting less than the advertised rate.

At the higher end, a Rs 1,500 cap on a 20% discount does not plateau until a bill of Rs 7,500, which covers most mid to upper restaurant spending. Cards with generous caps relative to their discount percentage tend to hold their value much better for regular dining.

Why banks advertise percentages instead of cap amounts

Because percentages are memorable and caps are not. "30% off at 200 restaurants" is a better marketing headline than "up to Rs 450 off at 200 restaurants." The first sounds unlimited. The second reveals exactly what you are getting.

This is not deceptive by itself because caps are disclosed in the terms, but it does mean most people are comparing cards on the number that matters least and ignoring the number that often determines real savings.

When caps are not published

Some offers in the dataset do not have a published cap. This can mean the offer genuinely has no cap, which is rare and valuable, or it can mean the cap exists in fine print that was not surfaced on the public pages used as sources.

The tool flags these cases rather than assuming an unlimited cap. If a ranking looks unusually strong for a card where no cap is shown, treat it with some skepticism and confirm the terms directly with the bank before relying on it.

Per-transaction vs. per-month caps

Some programs cap savings per transaction, others cap them per month across all transactions. Monthly caps matter if you use the card frequently. A card with a generous per-transaction cap but a tight monthly cap can feel great on the first few visits and then stop saving anything for the rest of the month.

Where monthly caps are published, they are included in the dataset. When they are not, the comparison assumes per-transaction behavior only.

Frequency limits matter too

Some restaurant offers are not just capped in rupees. They are also capped in frequency, for example once or twice per month per card. That matters a lot for heavy users. A card can look excellent on one bill and still stop being useful after your second visit.

This is one reason the tool is best used as a shortlist builder rather than a final source of record. If a card only saves you money on a limited number of visits, that condition belongs in your decision, not just the headline percentage and cap.
